Are there still possible combinations of musical notes?

Full chat on Sider

The problem in math terms

Whether musical possibilities are “used up” is a classic combinatorics question. In equal temperament we have 12 notes per octave (C, C#, D, … B).

How many melodies are possible?

For a short melody of only 10 notes, each slot can be any of 12 notes: 12^10 = 61,917,364,224 possibilities (over 61 billion).

  • Typical song (~50 notes): 12^50 ≈ 9.1 × 10^53 combinations
  • 100-note melody: 12^100 ≈ 8.3 × 10^107

More dimensions than pitch alone

Music is not just a note sequence — rhythm, dynamics, articulation, timbre, and harmony multiply the space.

Dimension Possible variables
Rhythm Note lengths (whole, half, quarter, eighth, …)
Dynamics Intensity (ppp through fff)
Articulation Staccato, legato, accent, …
Timbre Different instruments
Harmony Simultaneous chords

A conservative estimate

With only 5 rhythmic values and 7 dynamic levels per note, a 10-note melody has about (12 × 5 × 7)^10 ≈ (420)^10 ≈ 1.7 × 10^26 variants.

Compared to what humans have written

Estimates of songs ever written land around 100–200 million (≈ 2 × 10^8). That is roughly 1.2 × 10^-18 of the conservative 10-note space — a drop in the ocean.

Conclusion: we are nowhere near exhausting possibilities. Combinatorics gives a practically infinite space. What shapes what we hear is culture, preference for familiar scales and progressions, and human creativity — arrangement, instrumentation, and context can make “old” material feel new.
